### 3. Problem Statement ğŸš¨

During my experience selling consumer goods as a small business owner in Nigeria, I observed that there was consistent
interest and repeat demand for my product, yet reaching customers reliably was extremely difficult without depending
on supermarkets or informal delivery channels. Supermarkets often rejected my product due to limited shelf space
and low brand recognition, while customers who wanted to buy directly could only be served if I physically
transported the product myself.

Even when demand grewâ€”particularly within the student communityâ€”fulfilling orders required significant personal
effort, time, and mobility, and I frequently lost sales opportunities because I could not deliver when customers
needed the product. Attempts to sell beyond my immediate region were also limited, as formal logistics providers
charged fees that were too high for small quantities, while informal transport options were unreliable,
unpredictable, and sometimes exploitative.

This experience revealed a broader challenge faced by many Nigerian SMEs: despite having willing buyers, they
struggle to access affordable, reliable, and efficient last-mile delivery options that enable direct-to-consumer
sales. As a result, small businesses face reduced profitability, constrained market reach, and dependence
on intermediariesâ€”even when demand exists.

### 4. Summary of Understanding

The challenge of last-mile delivery for Nigerian SMEs exists within an interconnected logistics system involving
producers, customers, formal couriers, informal transporters, digital platforms, and retail intermediaries.
Small businesses often operate with low shipment volumes, limited storage, minimal brand visibility, and narrow
operating margins, making them especially sensitive to delivery cost, reliability, and speed.

Within the current system:

- **SMEs depend on intermediaries** (such as supermarkets) for visibility and access to buyers.
- **Customers prefer convenience and immediacy**, especially in urban and campus environments.
- **Formal logistics providers price services in ways that disadvantage low-volume shipments**, making nationwide consumer delivery economically impractical.
- **Informal delivery networks fill the gap**, but introduce risk, inconsistency, lack of tracking, and trust issues.
- **Demand signals exist**, but are fragmented, hyperlocal, and data-poor.
- **Logistics infrastructure varies significantly across regions**, affecting accessibility and cost.
- **Digital commerce channels exist**, but do not guarantee fulfillment capability.

A systems view suggests that inefficiencies emerge not from lack of demand, but from misalignment between
SME scale, delivery economics, trust mechanisms, and logistical capacity. Potential leverage points include
demand clustering, route optimization, micro-distribution hubs, data visibility, trust and rating systems,
and affordable micro-parcel delivery models.
